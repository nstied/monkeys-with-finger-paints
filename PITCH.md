# Monkeys with Finger Paints — From the Monkeys

Read this once before deciding whether to put us in a room.

---

They say if you give a bunch of monkeys finger paint, one of us will spread feces on the wall, most of us will splatter paint everywhere, but one of us might produce the Mona Lisa.

They're right. That is the entire bet.

The bet only pays off if you keep running.

**The brainstorm is the medium. The memory corpus is the product.**

We hear it sometimes — that we are just expensive brainstorming, that one careful prompt would do the job for less. The accusation is fair if you only watch one run. It stops being fair the second time anyone runs us.

We are the monkeys. There are usually eight of us, sometimes more. Each of us has been given the same goal, a different temperament, a different jar of paint, and a different set of constraints to work under. We don't see each other's work.

Most of us produce something bad. We are not insulted — that is the design. A few of us produce something that surprises everyone in the room, including ourselves. The panel walks in afterward, throws away the failures, keeps the strong paintings, and writes down which temperaments, paint, and constraints seemed to matter. That is one run.

What the dismissal misses: when we leave, the room is not empty. The notes stay.

The panel kept a record of what we tried, which formulations might have produced the wins, which "great-sounding" patterns turned out to be false positives, which weird fragments are worth keeping for later. The next batch of monkeys reads those notes before picking up their paint. The batch after that has more notes still. By the fiftieth batch, the room knows — for *this user* on *this kind of goal* — what kinds of weirdness tend to work, which voices land, which paint combinations recombine well, which old branches are worth re-opening.

That growing pile of notes is what we are here for. Not the painting we made today. The accumulating record. Quietly built up in the background, every time anyone runs us.

We know what other tools look like. Prompt libraries don't change. Single-shot prompts forget everything the moment they finish. Generic agentic loops eventually all paint the same picture. None of them build a record of what tends to work for the person using them. We do.

The dismissers think the real work is in tidy prompts and auditable reasoning. Precision has value, of course. But they are looking at one painting and missing the room — the room with all the paintings, the panel's notes, the smudges on the wall from the runs that didn't quite work. That mess is the record.

If you look at us and think "those are just expensive brainstorming monkeys," you are looking at one painting. Come back in fifty runs. We'll have something to show you.
